01 — Motivation

The Curse of Scale

Consider a deceptively simple question: given a new document, find all previously seen documents that are "similar" to it. This task — called nearest neighbor search or similarity search — is foundational to dozens of modern applications. Plagiarism detection, recommendation engines, image retrieval, duplicate web-page filtering, and bioinformatics all reduce to some form of it.

At small scale, the solution is trivial: compare the query against every item in your dataset, compute a similarity score, and return the top results. This brute-force approach is exact, simple to implement, and completely correct.

The problem? It does not scale.

With n items, brute-force comparison requires O(n²) operations. At the scale of the modern web — billions of documents — this becomes computationally impossible, even with the world's fastest hardware running in parallel.

— A fundamental bottleneck in large-scale data mining

The Numbers Tell the Story

Let's be concrete. Suppose you're building a news deduplication system. Each article comparison takes one microsecond. Here's how brute-force comparison time scales:

Number of Documents Pairs to Compare Time (@ 1 µs/pair) Feasible?
1,000 499,500 0.5 seconds ✓ Yes
10,000 49.9 million ~50 seconds ~ Slow
1,000,000 500 billion ~5.8 days ✗ No
1,000,000,000 5 × 10¹⁷ ~15,855 years ✗ Impossible

The root cause is the quadratic blowup. Every time you double the dataset, the number of comparisons quadruples. This is an inherent mathematical property of O(n²) algorithms — no amount of engineering optimization can escape it.

Why Not Use Trees?

A natural response is to reach for spatial data structures. KD-trees and ball-trees reduce exact nearest-neighbor search to O(log n) in low dimensions — a remarkable improvement. But they suffer from a well-documented pathology known as the curse of dimensionality.

In high-dimensional spaces (text documents have thousands of dimensions; images may have tens of thousands), spatial partitioning breaks down. The volume of space grows so rapidly that every point becomes approximately equidistant from every other point. KD-tree query time degrades toward O(n) — no better than brute force. Empirically, this degradation kicks in around 20–30 dimensions. Modern data often lives in hundreds or thousands of dimensions.

💡

The Key Insight We don't always need the exact nearest neighbor. In most real applications — recommendation, deduplication, clustering — an approximate nearest neighbor, found orders of magnitude faster, is perfectly acceptable. LSH formalizes this trade-off.

02 — Foundation

What is Locality Sensitive Hashing?

Locality Sensitive Hashing is a randomized algorithmic technique first formalized by Indyk and Motwani in their landmark 1998 paper "Approximate Nearest Neighbors: Towards Removing the Curse of Dimensionality." It was a fundamental breakthrough in the theory of approximate nearest neighbor search.

The Core Intuition

Standard cryptographic hash functions — like SHA-256 or MD5 — are designed to scatter inputs chaotically. Two nearly identical inputs produce completely different hashes. This is intentional: it prevents collision attacks and ensures security.

LSH inverts this design principle entirely. An LSH function is designed so that similar inputs are likely to hash to the same value, and dissimilar inputs are likely to hash to different values. Collisions become a signal, not noise.

P[ h(p) = h(q) ] = sim(p, q)

The probability of a hash collision equals the similarity of two points. This is the central property of LSH.

Formal Definition

Formally, a locality-sensitive hash family 𝐻 = {h : ℝᵈ → U} is a collection of hash functions such that for any two points p and q:

  • ①

    High-similarity collision

    If sim(p, q) ≥ s₁ (points are similar), then Pr[h(p) = h(q)] ≥ p₁ where p₁ is high.

  • ②

    Low-similarity separation

    If sim(p, q) ≤ s₂ (points are dissimilar), then Pr[h(p) = h(q)] ≤ p₂ where p₂ is low.

  • ③

    Separation condition

    Crucially, p₁ > p₂ and s₁ > s₂. The family must provide a meaningful gap between similar and dissimilar collision probabilities.

This gives us an (s₁, s₂, p₁, p₂)-sensitive hash family. The power of LSH comes from combining many such functions to amplify the gap between p₁ and p₂ — a process called amplification, which we'll explore in the Bands & Rows section.

Performance Guarantees

⚡

Sub-linear Query Time

LSH achieves O(n^ρ · d) query time, where ρ = log(1/p₁) / log(1/p₂) < 1. This is the fundamental speed-up over brute force.

Complexity
🎯

(ε, δ)-Approximation

Returns a neighbor within (1+ε) of the true nearest neighbor with probability at least 1-δ. Both parameters are tunable.

Accuracy
🔧

Tunable Trade-off

By adjusting hash table parameters, you explicitly control the precision-recall trade-off. More hash tables → higher recall, more space. Fewer → faster but more misses.

Flexibility
03 — Representation

Jaccard Similarity & Shingling

Before applying LSH to documents, we need two things: a way to measure similarity between documents, and a way to represent documents as mathematical objects that similarity can be computed on. This section covers both.

Jaccard Similarity

The Jaccard coefficient, named after Swiss botanist Paul Jaccard who introduced it in 1901 to compare plant species distributions, measures the similarity between two finite sets. Despite its age, it remains one of the most widely used similarity metrics in modern data mining.

J(A, B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B| ∈ [0, 1]

The fraction of elements in the union that are also in the intersection. Ranges from 0 (disjoint) to 1 (identical).

Intuition: Think of two sets as overlapping circles in a Venn diagram. Jaccard similarity is the ratio of the overlap area to the total area covered by both circles combined. Two identical sets have Jaccard similarity 1.0. Two completely disjoint sets have similarity 0.0.

📐

Quick Example
A = {cat, sat, on, mat, the}  |  B = {cat, hat, on, the, flat}
A ∩ B = {cat, on, the} → size 3
A ∪ B = {cat, sat, on, mat, the, hat, flat} → size 7
J(A, B) = 3/7 ≈ 0.429

K-Shingling: Documents as Sets

To apply Jaccard similarity to text documents, we need to convert each document into a set. The standard approach is k-shingling (also called k-gram or n-gram extraction).

A k-shingle is any contiguous sequence of k characters (or k words) appearing in the document. The shingle set of a document is the collection of all unique k-shingles it contains. By definition, a set has no duplicates — so even if the word "the" appears fifty times, it contributes only one shingle.

Python — K-Shingling 
def k_shingles(document: str, k: int) -> set:
    """Extract k-character shingles from a document."""
    # Normalize: lowercase, collapse whitespace
    doc = ' '.join(document.lower().split())
    return {doc[i:i+k] for i in range(len(doc) - k + 1)}

# Example
doc_a = "the cat sat on the mat"
doc_b = "the cat sat on the hat"

shingles_a = k_shingles(doc_a, k=3)
shingles_b = k_shingles(doc_b, k=3)

jaccard = len(shingles_a & shingles_b) / len(shingles_a | shingles_b)
print(f"Jaccard similarity: {jaccard:.4f}")
# Output: Jaccard similarity: 0.7500

Choosing k

The choice of k critically affects the quality of the shingling:

Small k (1–3 characters)

Very common shingles everywhere. Nearly every document will share most shingles with every other document — the feature is useless for discriminating similarity.

k = 9 characters (standard)

Recommended for large corpora. Long enough that most shingles are unique to specific text passages. Resistant to minor edits like punctuation changes.

Word k-shingles (k=2–3)

Use word boundaries instead of characters. k=2 bigrams work well for shorter documents and are interpretable. Better at capturing semantic proximity.

⚠

Practical Consideration Before shingling, always normalize your text: lowercase, remove punctuation, collapse whitespace. A minor formatting difference ("cat" vs "Cat") should not affect similarity scores. For web pages, strip HTML tags first.

04 — Signatures

MinHash Signatures

We can now represent documents as shingle sets and measure their similarity with Jaccard. But even as compressed set representations, comparing two large documents still requires iterating over potentially thousands of shingles. With millions of documents, this is still too slow. We need to compress the shingle sets into small, fixed-size signatures that preserve the Jaccard similarity.

MinHash — the minimum-hash signature scheme — is the elegant solution. Invented by Andrei Broder at AltaVista in 1997 to detect web duplicates, it compresses a set of arbitrary size into a fixed-length signature while mathematically guaranteeing that the similarity between signatures approximates the Jaccard similarity between the original sets.

The MinHash Function

Let U be the universe of all possible shingles. A MinHash function h corresponds to a random permutation π of U. Given a document's shingle set S ⊆ U, the MinHash value is:

hπ(S) = mins ∈ S { π(s) }

The minimum element of S under the random permutation π. This is the MinHash value.

In practice, generating true random permutations over a universe of billions of shingles is computationally expensive. Instead, we simulate permutations using random hash functions of the form h(x) = (ax + b) mod p, where a and b are random coefficients and p is a large prime. This is a standard, efficient approximation.

The Remarkable MinHash Theorem

Here is the central result that makes everything work — and it's worth pausing to appreciate its elegance:

P[ h(A) = h(B) ] = J(A, B) = |A∩B| / |A∪B|

For any random permutation π, the probability that two sets hash to the same value equals their Jaccard similarity. This is an exact equality, not an approximation.

🔑

Proof Sketch
Consider the union A ∪ B. A random permutation assigns a random ordering to all elements. The MinHash picks the element that comes first in this ordering. h(A) = h(B) if and only if this first element belongs to the intersection A ∩ B. Since each element is equally likely to be first, this probability is exactly |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B| = J(A, B). QED.

Building the Signature Matrix

We apply n independent MinHash functions to every document. The result is an n × m signature matrix where each row corresponds to a hash function and each column to a document. The signature for document d is the column vector of its n MinHash values.

Below is a concrete example with 3 documents, 7 shingles, and 3 hash functions. First, the characteristic matrix showing which shingles appear in which documents:

Implementation

Python — MinHash Implementation 
import hashlib, random

class MinHash:
    def __init__(self, n_hashes=128):
        self.n = n_hashes
        self._large_prime = 4294967311  # 2^32 + 15, a Mersenne-like prime
        # Random coefficients for n hash functions: h(x) = (ax + b) mod p
        self.a = [random.randint(1, self._large_prime - 1) for _ in range(n_hashes)]
        self.b = [random.randint(0, self._large_prime - 1) for _ in range(n_hashes)]

    def signature(self, shingle_set: set) -> list:
        """Compute the n-element MinHash signature for a shingle set."""
        sig = [float('inf')] * self.n
        for shingle in shingle_set:
            # Map shingle to integer via MD5
            x = int(hashlib.md5(shingle.encode()).hexdigest(), 16)
            for i in range(self.n):
                hval = (self.a[i] * x + self.b[i]) % self._large_prime
                sig[i] = min(sig[i], hval)
        return sig

    def estimate_jaccard(self, sig_a: list, sig_b: list) -> float:
        """Estimate Jaccard similarity from two signatures."""
        matches = sum(a == b for a, b in zip(sig_a, sig_b))
        return matches / self.n
📊

Estimation Error
With n MinHash functions, the standard error of the Jaccard estimate is σ = √(J(1-J)/n). For Jaccard = 0.5 and n = 100, this gives σ ≈ 0.05 — a 5% error. For n = 400, σ ≈ 2.5%. The trade-off is memory: 400 64-bit hashes per document costs 3.2 KB per document.

05 — Amplification

The Bands & Rows Technique

We now have compact signatures for every document. But we still face the fundamental problem: with m documents, comparing all pairs of signatures is still O(m²). We need a way to quickly identify candidate pairs — without comparing everything.

The Bands and Rows technique is the algorithmic heart of LSH. It transforms the problem of finding similar pairs into a bucketing problem.

The Algorithm

  1. 1

    Partition the signature matrix into bands

    Divide the n-row signature matrix into b bands, each containing r rows (so n = b × r). Each band corresponds to a subset of hash functions.

  2. 2

    Hash each band's sub-vector

    For each band, take the r-element sub-vector of each document's signature and hash it into a bucket. Two documents with identical sub-vectors in a band land in the same bucket.

  3. 3

    Identify candidate pairs

    Any two documents that share a bucket in at least one band become candidate pairs. They are flagged for further examination. Documents sharing no buckets in any band are rejected without comparison.

  4. 4

    Verify candidates

    Compute the exact Jaccard similarity (or another metric) only for candidate pairs. This is a tiny fraction of all O(m²) pairs — the dramatic saving that makes LSH practical.

The Probability Analysis

What is the probability that a pair of documents with Jaccard similarity s becomes a candidate pair? Let's work it through:

Two documents agree on a single MinHash value with probability s. They agree on all r rows of a band with probability sʳ. They do NOT share a bucket in any single band with probability 1 - sʳ. They do NOT share a bucket in ANY of the b bands with probability (1 - sʳ)ᵇ. Therefore:

P[candidate] = 1 − ( 1 − sr )b

s = Jaccard similarity, b = number of bands, r = rows per band, n = b × r total hashes

This function produces an S-shaped probability curve — nearly 0 for low similarity, then rising steeply near a threshold t ≈ (1/b)^(1/r), reaching nearly 1 for high similarity. This is exactly what we want: near-certain detection of truly similar pairs, near-certain rejection of dissimilar ones.

Choosing b and r

More bands (↑b)

Lower threshold — catches more similar pairs. Higher recall. But more false positives (dissimilar pairs that end up as candidates). More memory for hash tables.

More rows per band (↑r)

Higher threshold — only very similar pairs become candidates. Fewer false positives. Risk of missing true near-duplicates. Sharper transition in the S-curve.

Practical rule of thumb

Set t = (1/b)^(1/r) to your desired similarity threshold. Common choices: b=20, r=5 (n=100) for t≈0.5; or b=25, r=4 (n=100) for t≈0.45.

Complete Worked Example

Let's trace the entire LSH pipeline from raw text to similarity detection, using a concrete example of finding near-duplicate news articles.

The Three Articles

📰

Article A

"Scientists discover new species of deep-sea fish in the Pacific Ocean near the Mariana Trench"

Article C

Step-by-Step Pipeline

1

Extract 3-word shingles

Article A shingles (sample): {scientists discover new, discover new species, new species of, species of deep, of deep-sea fish, deep-sea fish in, fish in the, in the pacific, the pacific ocean, pacific ocean near, ocean near the, near the mariana, the mariana trench}

Article B shingles (sample): {scientists discover new, discover new species, new species of, species of deep, of deep-sea creature, deep-sea creature in, creature in the, in the pacific, the pacific ocean, ...}

A ∩ B = {scientists discover new, discover new species, new species of, species of deep, in the pacific, the pacific ocean, pacific ocean near, ocean near the, near the mariana, the mariana trench} → 10 shingles

A ∪ B = 10 + 3 (A only) + 3 (B only) = 16 shingles

J(A, B) = 10/16 = 0.625 — highly similar ✓

J(A, C) ≈ 0.02 — nearly disjoint (1–2 common shingles out of 40+) ✓

2

Compute MinHash signatures (n = 100)

Apply 100 independent hash functions h₁...h₁₀₀ to each shingle set. Each document gets a 100-element signature vector. Since J(A,B) = 0.625, we expect approximately 62 of the 100 MinHash values to agree between A and B.

Since J(A,C) ≈ 0.02, we expect only about 2 of 100 values to agree between A and C.

3

Apply Bands & Rows (b = 20, r = 5)

The threshold is t ≈ (1/20)^(1/5) ≈ 0.549. Since J(A,B) = 0.625 > 0.549, A and B should be flagged as candidates with high probability.

Probability A & B become candidates:
P = 1 − (1 − 0.625⁵)²⁰ = 1 − (1 − 0.095)²⁰ ≈ 1 − (0.905)²⁰ ≈ 1 − 0.13 ≈ 0.87
87% chance they share at least one band bucket. ✓ (With n=200, this rises to >99%)

Probability A & C become candidates:
P = 1 − (1 − 0.02⁵)²⁰ ≈ 1 − (1 − 3.2×10⁻⁹)²⁰ ≈ 6.4×10⁻⁸
Essentially zero. A and C are never compared. ✓

4

Verify candidates

A and B are flagged as candidates. We compute their exact Jaccard similarity: 0.625. This exceeds our deduplication threshold (say, 0.5). They are marked as near-duplicates.

A and C are never even compared. The entire second comparison is skipped — this is the saving LSH provides. At a million-document scale, we would skip billions of comparisons.

🎯

The Key Result
Out of 3 possible pairs, LSH correctly identified 1 candidate (A,B) and skipped 2 (A,C) and (B,C). At scale with 1 million documents, brute force requires 500 billion comparisons. LSH with the same parameters would generate roughly O(n^ρ) candidates — potentially millions instead of billions.

False Positives and False Negatives

LSH is an approximate method, so two types of errors can occur:

False Positives

  • Dissimilar documents that happen to share a bucket in at least one band
  • Cost: unnecessary verification computations
  • Controlled by: increasing r (fewer, longer bands)
  • Generally cheap — exact verification quickly discards them

False Negatives

  • Similar documents that fail to share any bucket in any band
  • Cost: missed near-duplicates — true positives we never find
  • Controlled by: increasing b or n (more hashes)
  • More serious — these are the pairs we were trying to find
07 — Generalization

LSH Families for Other Metrics

MinHash is one specific LSH family, designed for the Jaccard / set-similarity metric. The LSH framework, however, is a general principle that has been extended to cover virtually every major similarity metric used in data mining and machine learning. Different data modalities require different LSH families.

Jaccard

MinHash — Set Similarity

The original LSH family. Converts sets to integer signatures via random permutations. The probability of collision equals the Jaccard similarity.

Hash Function
h_π(S) = min{π(s) : s ∈ S}
Collision Probability
P[h(A) = h(B)] = J(A, B)
Use Cases
Document deduplication, web crawl deduplication, plagiarism detection, collaborative filtering on item sets
Variant: SimHash
Google's variant using term frequencies, not just presence/absence. Powers search deduplication at web scale.
Cosine

Random Projection (Sign Random Projection)

For real-valued vectors (word embeddings, image features, user preference vectors), the angle between vectors — measured by cosine similarity — is the natural metric. Random Projection LSH hashes based on which side of a random hyperplane a vector lies on.

Hash Function
h_r(x) = sign(r · x) where r is a random Gaussian vector
Collision Probability
P[h(x) = h(y)] = 1 − θ(x,y) / π where θ is the angle between x and y
Use Cases
Word2Vec/BERT embedding search, image similarity, audio fingerprinting, neural network output search
Intuition
Vectors pointing in similar directions are likely on the same side of most random hyperplanes. More similar → more often same side → higher collision probability.
Python — Random Projection LSH 
import numpy as np

class RandomProjectionLSH:
    def __init__(self, d: int, n_hashes: int):
        # d = vector dimension, generate n random Gaussian hyperplanes
        self.planes = np.random.randn(n_hashes, d)  # shape: (n, d)

    def hash(self, x: np.ndarray) -> tuple:
        """Hash a vector to a binary signature."""
        projections = self.planes @ x  # shape: (n,)
        return tuple((projections > 0).astype(int))

    def cosine_similarity(self, a, b):
        return np.dot(a, b) / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))

# Example: find similar product embeddings
lsh = RandomProjectionLSH(d=768, n_hashes=64)  # 768-d BERT embeddings
buckets = {}
for product_id, embedding in product_catalog.items():
    key = lsh.hash(embedding)
    buckets.setdefault(key, []).append(product_id)
Euclidean

p-Stable Distribution LSH

For Euclidean distance (ℓ₂ norm), we use hash functions based on p-stable distributions — specifically the Gaussian distribution. This family was introduced by Datar et al. (2004) and underpins many image retrieval and clustering applications.

Hash Function
h_{a,b}(x) = ⌊(a·x + b) / w⌋ — a random Gaussian direction a, random offset b ∈ [0,w)
Collision Probability
Monotonically decreasing in ‖p−q‖₂. Controlled by bucket width w.
Use Cases
Image retrieval by pixel features, audio fingerprinting, 3D point cloud matching, geographic coordinate search
Key Parameter
Bucket width w: larger w → more collisions → lower threshold. Must be tuned to the characteristic distance scale of your data.
Hamming

Bit Sampling LSH

For binary strings, Hamming distance counts the number of bit positions where strings differ. The LSH family for Hamming distance is elegantly simple: randomly sample k individual bit positions and check if the strings agree on all of them.

Hash Function
h_i(x) = xᵢ — simply read bit i. Two strings collide if they agree on all k sampled bits.
Collision Probability
P[h(x) = h(y)] = 1 − d_H(x,y)/n for length-n strings
Use Cases
DNA sequence similarity (ACGT → binary encoding), error-correcting codes, binary image hashing, network packet analysis
Advantage
Extremely computationally cheap — no multiplication or division needed, just bit reads. Ideal for hardware implementation.

09 — Analysis

Advantages & Limitations

LSH is a powerful technique, but like all algorithms, it comes with specific trade-offs that must be understood before deployment. Here is a structured analysis of its strengths and weaknesses.

✓ Advantages

  • Sub-linear query time O(n^ρ) with ρ < 1 — the fundamental theoretical guarantee
  • Scales to billions of items — used in production at Google, Netflix, and Amazon
  • Tunable precision-recall trade-off via b and r parameters
  • Works in high-dimensional spaces where tree-based methods fail completely
  • Multiple LSH families cover Jaccard, cosine, Euclidean, and Hamming metrics
  • Preprocessing can be parallelized trivially — embarrassingly parallel
  • Sub-linear space possible with streaming variants
  • Probabilistic guarantees — not just a heuristic

✗ Limitations

  • Approximate results — false negatives (missed pairs) are inherent
  • Parameter tuning (k, b, r, n) requires domain knowledge
  • High memory overhead: multiple hash tables, large signature matrices
  • O(n·d) preprocessing cost — non-trivial for very large, high-dimensional datasets
  • Different metrics require different LSH families — no single implementation
  • Difficult to handle dynamic datasets efficiently (insertions/deletions)
  • Theoretical guarantees assume uniformly random data — real data may behave differently

Modern Practice
In practice, for dense vector embeddings (from neural networks), HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) graphs have largely replaced LSH as the method of choice due to their superior empirical performance. However, for sparse set-based similarity (text deduplication, collaborative filtering on sparse rating matrices), MinHash LSH remains the gold standard. Understanding LSH provides the theoretical foundation for all approximate nearest neighbor methods.

10 — Takeaways

Conclusion & Further Reading

Locality Sensitive Hashing represents a paradigm shift in how we think about similarity search. By deliberately engineering hash collisions to correlate with similarity — and then using probabilistic amplification to separate signal from noise — it transforms an intractable O(n²) problem into a tractable sub-linear one.

The elegance of LSH lies in the MinHash theorem: a random permutation of the shingle universe produces a collision probability that exactly equals Jaccard similarity. This isn't an approximation — it's an exact mathematical equality. The approximation comes only from using a finite number of hash functions as an estimator. More functions → better estimate. The trade-off between memory, computation, and accuracy is explicit and controllable.

The Bands and Rows technique then amplifies this signal into a practical data structure: an S-shaped acceptance curve that catches similar pairs with high probability while discarding dissimilar ones almost certainly. By tuning b and r, practitioners can precisely target any desired similarity threshold.

The beauty of LSH is that it turns a collision — normally something to avoid — into the mechanism of discovery. Similar things find each other by landing in the same bucket. The algorithm doesn't search for similarity; it creates the conditions under which similarity reveals itself.

Key Takeaways

🧮

The Scaling Problem

Brute-force O(n²) similarity search fails at web scale. LSH achieves sub-linear query time O(n^ρ) with formal probabilistic guarantees.

🔄

Shingling + MinHash

Documents become shingle sets. MinHash converts sets to compact signatures while preserving Jaccard similarity with mathematical precision.

📊

Bands Amplify Signal

The b×r banding scheme creates an S-shaped probability curve with a tuneable threshold at t ≈ (1/b)^(1/r).

🌍

Production at Scale

LSH (and its variants) power deduplication, recommendations, music recognition, and embedding search across billions of items daily.
